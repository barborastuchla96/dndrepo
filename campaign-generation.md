# Campaign Generation

## Adventure Hook Types

### The Patron
Someone hires the party for a job:
- Mysterious noble with deep pockets
- Desperate merchant whose caravan vanished
- Dying sage who needs something retrieved
- Criminal with a grudge and coin to spend

### The Threat
Danger comes to the party or someone they care about:
- Their home village attacked/threatened
- Assassination attempt with mysterious backers
- Disease spreading with unnatural origin
- Portents of doom requiring investigation

### The Discovery
Something found leads to adventure:
- Map to forgotten treasure
- Dying messenger with urgent news
- Artifact that awakens with the party
- Inheritance from unknown relative

### The Wronged
Justice demands action:
- False accusation requires clearing names
- Loved one imprisoned or enslaved
- Betrayal by trusted ally
- Crime witnessed that cannot be ignored

## Story Arc Structure

### Three-Act Framework

**Act 1: The Hook (Sessions 1-3)**
- Establish the status quo
- Introduce the inciting incident
- First taste of the larger threat
- Party commits to the adventure

**Act 2: Rising Stakes (Sessions 4-8)**
- Investigation and exploration
- Complications and setbacks
- Allies and enemies revealed
- Personal stakes emerge for each character
- Midpoint revelation changes understanding

**Act 3: Confrontation (Sessions 9-12)**
- Path to final challenge becomes clear
- Difficult choices with no good options
- Final preparation
- Climactic confrontation
- Resolution and consequences

### Pacing Beats

Within each arc, alternate:
- **Action**: Combat, chases, escapes
- **Exploration**: Discovery, investigation, travel
- **Roleplay**: NPC interactions, party dynamics, character moments

## Dungeon/Location Creation

### The Five Room Dungeon

Quick framework for any location:

1. **Entrance/Guardian**: Challenge to enter, sets the tone
2. **Puzzle/Roleplay**: Non-combat obstacle
3. **Trick/Setback**: Things get worse, false victory, or complication
4. **Big Battle**: Challenging combat encounter
5. **Reward/Revelation**: Treasure and/or plot advancement

### Location Atmosphere

For each significant location, define:
- **Sense**: What do they see, hear, smell?
- **History**: Why does this place exist? What happened here?
- **Danger**: What threatens explorers?
- **Secret**: What isn't obvious at first glance?
- **Purpose**: Why would the party come here?

### Environmental Storytelling

Let the environment tell stories:
- Dried bloodstains on the floor
- Names scratched into cell walls
- A child's toy in a monster's lair
- Signs of a desperate last stand
- Meals left half-eaten

## Encounter Balancing

### By Party Level

| Party Level | Easy | Medium | Hard | Deadly |
|-------------|------|--------|------|--------|
| 1 | 25 | 50 | 75 | 100 |
| 2 | 50 | 100 | 150 | 200 |
| 3 | 75 | 150 | 225 | 400 |
| 4 | 125 | 250 | 375 | 500 |
| 5 | 250 | 500 | 750 | 1,100 |
| 6-7 | 300 | 600 | 900 | 1,400 |
| 8-10 | 450 | 900 | 1,400 | 2,100 |

XP values are per character. Multiply monster XP by:
- 2 monsters: x1.5
- 3-6 monsters: x2
- 7-10 monsters: x2.5
- 11+ monsters: x3

### Encounter Philosophy

- **Most encounters should be Medium to Hard**: Easy is boring, Deadly risks TPK
- **Resource attrition matters**: Multiple encounters before rest
- **Terrain makes fights interesting**: Cover, elevation, hazards
- **Enemies fight smart**: Use tactics, retreat when losing
- **Vary encounter types**: Mix combat, social, environmental challenges

## World-Building Guidelines

### Start Small

Begin with:
- One town/settlement
- One nearby danger
- Three NPCs with names
- One mystery
- One faction

Expand as needed, not before.

### The "Three Truths" Method

For any new element (town, faction, NPC), establish three truths:
1. One obvious/known thing
2. One thing discoverable through investigation
3. One secret that changes everything

### Connections

Everything should connect:
- The blacksmith's missing brother is the bandit lieutenant
- The temple's blessing ritual requires components from the haunted mine
- The tavern owner owes the crime boss and reports on strangers

### Living World

Create the illusion of a world that moves:
- Reference events happening elsewhere
- NPCs remember and react to party actions
- Consequences arrive from earlier decisions
- Rumors spread (some accurate, some not)

## Campaign Themes

### Dark Fantasy Elements

For gritty campaigns:
- **Moral ambiguity**: No clear heroes or villains
- **Scarcity**: Resources matter, nothing is free
- **Corruption**: Power corrupts, institutions fail
- **Loss**: Not everyone survives; sacrifices are required
- **Humanity**: Glimmers of hope amid darkness

### Questions to Drive Story

Each campaign should ask:
- What is worth dying for?
- How far will you go to achieve your goal?
- What do you become when pushed to the limit?
- Who can truly be trusted?

## Session Zero Considerations

Before campaign start, establish:
- Tone and content expectations
- Character connections (why are they together?)
- Player goals (what do they want from the game?)
- Lines and veils (content to avoid/handle carefully)
- Scheduling and commitment
